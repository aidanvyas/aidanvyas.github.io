I plan to spend my career working on empirical asset pricing and artificial intelligence, but I am also interested in politics, longevity, sports, and food.

I would like to provide a defense of why one should spend their lives focused on AI and asset pricing. The case for AI is rather self-explanatory and non-controversial. Artificial intelligence is simply that––artificial intelligence. The hope is that as we approach artificial general intelligence (AGI), anything that humans can do now machines will be able to do. The main advantages of machines is that they are cheaper, faster, and potentially smarter than human beings.

Most people intuitively understand that ChatGPT can draft a cheaper and faster (and perhaps better) essay than the average writer. And as artificial intelligence continues to improve, the scope of problems that it can tackle widens. I believe that AI has the capability to help us solve all of physics, cure every disease, explore the galaxy, and deliver world peace––all in my lifetime––and I hope to contribute to this progress.

The case for working on empirical asset pricing is less obvious, but arguably just as important. Firstly, what is empirical asset pricing? Asset pricing is simply the study of why assets are priced the way that they are. In essence, why is the S&P 500 at 5,000 instead of 500 or 50,000? And baked into the question of prices is why returns are what they are. Why does the S&P 500 return 12% instead of 1.2% or 120%? And empirical just means that we care more about results than theories.

Over the last century, short-term interest rates have averaged around 3%, so if at the beginning of each year you took out a loan and bought the S&P 500, you would make 9% a year on average. If you borrowed $100, you would make $9. And if you borrowed $1 billion, you would have made $90 million. Now why doesn't everyone do this? How is this not an infinite money glitch?

Well for one the S&P 500 is a risky investment. Its returns are not always 12%. The standard deviation (or volatility) of annual returns is 20%, meaning that annual returns can reasonably be expected to range from -29% to 51%. In the case where returns are negative and you borrowed money, you would owe your lender a large shortfall.

And secondly, the worst case scenarios for the S&P typically coincide with downturns in the real economy. So not only do you run the risk of losing money some years, you lose money precisely when you need it the most (e.g., the 2008 financial crisis).

Diving deeper, most of the field focused on cross-sectional asset pricing, or how certain assets are priced relative to each other and what this says about their future returns. For instance, it is widely known that Warren Buffett has had an exemplary investing track record and part of the reason for his outperformance is value investing––buying stocks that are cheap relative to their fundamentals.

Why does value investing work so well? Perhaps it's because value stocks are more risky, i.e., the returns of these companies vary more widely than the overall market or that they are in greater danger during bad economic times. Or perhaps it's because they are simply mispriced because investors would rather boast about owning tech companies compared to textile or tobacco companies at cocktail parties. Or perhaps it's some combination of the two.

Two groups of the financial services industry that deserve particular defense are long-short equity managers and market makers (high-frequency traders) who make money via identifying mispricings and bearing risks, respectively. Most people understand if you wanted to sell your house within a month, you would probably get something close to fair market value. However, if you wanted to sell your house within a minute, you would get pennies on the dollars.

This is obviously a suboptimal situation driven by the illiquidity of the housing market. A world where you could get 99.9% of your home's fair value in a minute would obviously be beneficial to you, and the 0.1% spread is what market makers collect in exchange for accepting the risk of adverse selection––e.g., you might be selling your house because you have inside information on the quality of the roofing or foundation.

The case for long-short equity managers is a bit more opaque. The act of shorting a stock is often thought of as unethical, but in reality, it helps make markets less volatile (leading to less frequent bubbles and downturns) and can help to expose frauds. However, the biggest benefit of these investors is that they help to set prices efficiently in the market.

Why does this matter? Why does it matter if a stock is mispriced or a market is inefficient? Well prices are the mechanism in which business owners can access capital. If prices are too high relative to the fundamentals, then capital is wasted on fads, and if prices are too low, then valuable projects go underfunded. An extreme antithesis of an efficient market is a communist society where the government sets all prices, leading to fewer innovations and a lower standard of living for all.

The most convincing argument against working in finance is that almost everyone who works in finance is extremely smart and society would be better served if we expanded our talents on more useful endeavours––like developing new medicines or creating new inventions. And my counterargument to this is that efficient capital markets allow very best scientists to raise the money on the best possible terms, supporting innovation economy-wide.

While I don't view empirical asset pricing and artificial intelligence to be naturally connected, I do believe that if we were able to successfully apply AI to finance, we could help the market generate more efficient prices, leading to a more just and efficient allocation of resources, which would benefit all of society.
